Key Takeaways

Everything you just proved you knew:

📜 MODULE 1 — The Road to Monetary Union
Two motivations: economic (remove exchange-rate friction in the single market) and political (currency as symbol of integration). The Werner Report (1969) was the first attempt but collapsed with Bretton Woods (1971). The Snake (1972) tried to keep currencies within ±2.25% but collapsed after the oil shock (1973) — becoming a DM zone. The EMS (1979) and its Exchange Rate Mechanism succeeded from 1983 onward thanks to France's "competitive disinflation" and the Deutschmark effect. The Delors Report (1988) paved the way to Maastricht.
⚖️ MODULE 2 — The Maastricht Treaty
Signed 1991, ratified 1992. Transformed the EEC into the EU. Three stages toward EMU, culminating January 1st 1999. Five convergence criteria: inflation ≤ 1.5% above 3 lowest; long-term rates ≤ 2% above 3 lowest; exchange rate stability ≥ 2 years; debt ≤ 60% GDP; deficit ≤ 3% GDP. Only Luxembourg met all 5 in 1991. Strong German influence: ECB modelled on Bundesbank, HQ in Frankfurt. A "two-speed Europe": core EMU members vs. opt-outs (UK, Denmark).
🏛️ MODULE 3 — ESCB vs. Eurosystem & Structure
ESCB = ECB + all EU NCBs (including non-euro). Eurosystem = ECB + euro-area NCBs only. Three bodies: Governing Council (sets rates & policy), Executive Board (daily ops, 8-year non-renewable terms), General Council (coordinates all EU NCBs). Full institutional independence — no government instructions allowed. ECB is supranational; NCBs keep national legal personality.
🎯 MODULE 4 — Price Stability, Independence & Infrastructure
Primary objective: HICP below but close to 2% over the medium term. Two-pillar strategy: Economic Analysis (short-term: wages, activity, financial conditions) cross-checked with Monetary Analysis (long-term: money supply & prices). Four types of independence: institutional, personal, functional, financial. TARGET2: real-time euro settlement system — instant, central bank money, reduces systemic risk. Euro: 7 banknote denominations (€5–€500), 8 coin denominations (1 cent–€2).
